The big idea · 1 of 2

A chatbot answers. An agent runs a process.

Ask a plain chatbot "how much vacation can I carry over?" and it produces words that sound right. It might be right. You can't tell, it can't prove it, and nobody is accountable if it's wrong. That is not good enough for HR.

The system you'll use today is different. Every question runs a scenario chain — a fixed, auditable sequence of steps, like a well-run case-handling process:

  • It looks up your record — and only yours.
  • It finds the actual policy and quotes its source — it is not allowed to answer from memory.
  • It checks the exceptions that apply to you (your state, your tenure, your balances).
  • It passes a checkpoint where a human can be involved — always at the same step.
  • It writes down every step it took in an audit trail anyone can review.
  • And it counts toward a business measure someone owns — like "fewer HR tickets".
Key phrase to remember: "the chain is the unit of value, not the model." The AI model is one worker inside a governed process — the process is what you're buying, trusting, and measuring.

Five slots that make a use case real

Business objective → Use case → Person served → The chain → Measure moved "cut HR ticket load" policy questions Raj (employee) look up → ground → ticket deflection Sofia (HR Ops) check → decide → 0% → 55% ★checkpoint → act

Every scenario in this module fills in all five slots. That is the test you should apply to any AI idea in your own team:

  1. Business objective — what outcome are we actually chasing?
  2. Use case — which specific, repeatable request does this handle?
  3. Person served — a named role who asks, and a named role who owns the result.
  4. The chain — the governed steps, including where a human checks in.
  5. Measure moved — the number that proves it worked, with an owner.
you can take an HR idea from your own experience ("automate reference letters") and fill all five slots out loud. If a slot is empty, the idea isn't ready — that's the finding, not a failure.

How a question travels

Seven stops from question to answer

Behind the scenes there is a 24-step enterprise blueprint; the part you'll watch today is the seven stops every single request makes. In plain language:

StopWhat happensPlain-English question it answers
1 · Look upReads the asker's own record — never anyone else's"Who is asking, and what's their situation?"
2 · GroundFinds the actual policy or document, with its source"What do our rules actually say?"
3 · CheckApplies the exceptions: state law, tenure, balances, deadlines"What applies to this person specifically?"
4 · DecideDrafts the proposed answer or action — with a confidence score"What should we say or do, and how sure are we?"
5 · ★ CheckpointThe human control point — the same step, every time"Does a person need to look at this first?"
6 · ActDelivers the answer, generates the letter, files the request"Do it — but only if the checkpoint approved."
7 · LearnRecords gaps and outcomes so the process improves"What should we fix for next time?"
The one rule that never bends: stop 6 (Act) is impossible to reach without passing stop 5 (the checkpoint). That isn't a policy the AI follows — it's how the machinery is built. The AI cannot skip it any more than an elevator can skip its door sensor.
The checkpoint

Four settings of human oversight — a risk dial, not an on/off switch

SettingWho's involvedUsed when…Example
TOUCH-FREENo one — but every step is still recordedReading information, answering standard questions"How much vacation can I carry over?"
CONFIRMThe requester confirms before it proceedsSomething gets created or changed, mildly"Generate my employment letter" — you review it first
ESCALATEAutomatic when confident; a specialist when notThe AI might not be able to resolve itA pay question the policy library can't answer → routed to Payroll, with your conversation attached
APPROVEA named human must approve before anything happensConsequences are seriousExtending a job offer (used in other HR areas, not Tier-0)

The design question you'll practice today: for each HR process, which setting fits — and what concrete harm justifies anything stricter than touch-free? Too little oversight is unaccountable. Too much, and the humans become the bottleneck the automation was meant to remove.

Reframe to internalize: when the AI escalates, it has not failed. A precisely-routed handover with full context is a success — it moves the "misrouted tickets" measure from 23% toward under 3%. The failure mode is guessing.
The receipt trail

If a step didn't write a receipt, it didn't happen

Every stop on the journey writes one row to an audit ledger — fourteen facts, always the same fourteen. Together they let an HR leader, an auditor, or a regulator replay any answer the AI ever gave:

  • Which run — a unique id for this one question
  • When — timestamp of each step
  • Which scenario — e.g. Policy Q&A
  • Which step & stage — where in the journey
  • Which agent — the AI worker that acted
  • For whom — the person served
  • What it did & the evidence — in readable words
  • How confident — the score the checkpoint used
  • Checkpoint setting — touch-free / confirm / escalate / approve
  • Who approved — "system", or the human's name
  • Outcome — ok · approved · confirmed · escalated · withheld
Twice the value: the same receipts that satisfy an auditor also teach the system — unanswered questions reveal gaps in the policy library; escalation patterns retune when to hand over. Evidence and improvement are one habit, not two.
in Activity 1 you can point at the ledger on screen and narrate a run to your neighbor: "here it looked him up, here's the policy it quoted, here's the checkpoint, here's the proof no human was needed."

Scenario briefs · 1 of 4

Policy Q&A Copilot — scenarios 1, 2, 3

1 · Policy Q&A — "How many unused vacation days can I carry over?"

Most HR tickets are policy questions with a known answer. The walk: it reads Raj's record (California, 3 years' tenure, 14.5 days banked) → finds the PTO policy and quotes it → notices California's special rule applies to him → answers precisely, citing the source document → touch-free checkpoint → delivered in Teams. No ticket ever exists. If it couldn't find a grounding policy, it says so and records a gap — it never improvises.

2 · Status lookup — "Where is my tuition reimbursement request?"

A top ticket driver, and a pure read: it finds his request, reports the stage ("Manager approval"), the owner, and the expected date. Requests stuck too long automatically nudge their owner. Notably: no AI creativity involved at all — a reminder that not everything needs a language model.

3 · Knowledge search — "Is there a stipend for home-office equipment?"

Sometimes people want the document, not an answer. This returns the right policies, ranked, with the matching passage quoted — and searches only what the asker is allowed to read.

Measures moved: ticket deflection 0%→55% · first-contact resolution 61%→89% · search success 48%→85% (synthetic figures)

Scenario briefs · 2 of 4

Life-Event Navigator — scenarios 4, 5

4 · Life-event guidance — "We just had a baby! What do I need to do?"

A birth triggers a web of deadlines most employees discover too late: elect parental leave within 30 days, add the child to benefits within the 31-day special window, update withholding, verify the dependent. The Navigator recognizes the event, assembles every triggered action, and lays them out in deadline order, personalized (California pay top-up for Raj; different rules in Germany). It can start the tasks for him. Unusual situations hand over to Sofia — with everything attached.

5 · Leave & absence — "I need time off to care for my father after surgery."

Leave is where policy complexity meets personal anxiety. The chain identifies the leave type (caregiver), grounds on the caregiver policy, then computes his option stack: 12 weeks job-protected leave, California's paid family leave running alongside, his 6 sick days first, then vacation balance. It answers with the steps and starts a draft leave case — at a moment when the last thing he needs is a form maze.

Measures moved: life-event completion 62%→95% · leave-question deflection 0%→60% · employee satisfaction 3.6→4.5 (synthetic figures)

Scenario briefs · 3 of 4

Letter & Doc Generation — scenarios 6, 7 the CONFIRM checkpoint

6 · Letters — "I need an employment verification letter for my mortgage."

High volume, fully templated, yet still manual in most HR shops. The chain picks the right template, validates his record is complete first (a letter with missing data never ships — it fixes the data first, see scenario 7), drafts the letter, and shows it to Raj. He confirms — that's the checkpoint — then it's issued and logged to the audit trail. Three days of turnaround becomes under a minute.

7 · Data corrections — "My address is wrong — it should be…"

Bad records poison everything downstream — letters, taxes, benefits. The chain detects the personal data in his message (and handles it under privacy controls), structures the change as current value → proposed value, shows him exactly what will change, and — after he confirms — files it with the owning data team and its service-level clock. He can track it with scenario 2 afterward.

Why CONFIRM and not touch-free? Both scenarios change something in the world. The human confirms what will happen — but doesn't redo the work. Discussion for Activity 4: what concrete harm would touch-free letters enable?

Measures moved: document turnaround 3 days→instant · self-serve rate 5%→80% · intake service-level adherence 71%→98% (synthetic)

Scenario briefs · 4 of 4

Ticket Deflection — scenarios 8, 9 the ESCALATE checkpoint

8 · Smart routing — "My paycheck was garnished incorrectly — who do I talk to?"

The most important scenario in the module, because it shows the AI knowing what it doesn't know. Court-ordered pay matters are deliberately not in the policy library — they need a specialist. The chain searches, finds nothing solid, scores its confidence near zero, and the checkpoint fires: instead of guessing, it routes to Payroll Operations with the full conversation attached and tells Raj exactly who will follow up and when. He never repeats himself; Payroll never gets a misrouted mystery.

9 · Multilingual — "¿Cuántos días de vacaciones me quedan este año?"

Lucía in Mexico City asks in Spanish. The chain detects the language, runs the exact same grounded process, and answers in Spanish — her balance, the policy source, everything. The lesson: language is a wrapper around the chain, not a separate system. One governed process, every language.

What "deliberately not in the library" teaches: deciding what the AI may answer is a governance choice HR owns — a coverage decision, not a technical accident. Sensitive topics are route-to-a-human by design.

Measures moved: deflection 0%→55% · misrouted tickets 23%→under 3% · non-English satisfaction 3.1→4.5 (synthetic figures)

What the AI is allowed to know

The policy library: 30 documents, deliberately curated

The AI answers only from a governed library — in this module, 30 synthetic documents: 15 policies (vacation, parental leave, caregiver leave, sick leave, holidays, benefits, 401(k), expenses, accommodations, relocation…), 10 how-to and life-event guides, 3 FAQs, and 2 documents in Spanish and German.

Every document carries a label set

  • What it is — policy, guide, or FAQ
  • Where it applies — global, California, Germany…
  • What language it's in
  • Which scenarios it supports — and a safeguard fails if any scenario ever loses its supporting documents

Searchable two ways

  • By words — classic keyword matching, fully explainable
  • By meaning — every passage also has a mathematical "fingerprint" (a vector), so "time away when my mother is ill" can find the caregiver policy even with no words in common. In production this runs on Azure AI Search.
The gaps are the governance: garnishment and court-order topics are excluded on purpose (that's why scenario 8 escalates), and sensitive topics appear only as "contact this team" entries. Curating this library — what's in, what's out, what routes to a human — is an HR job, and after this module it could be yours.
Behind the scenes

How it works in production — six layers, no jargon

1Where facts are born Workday (people), SuccessFactors (legacy regions), ServiceNow (HR requests) — the official systems of record.
2The refinery Copies land raw, get cleaned and standardized, and personal identifiers are masked before anything else sees them. Only the final "agent-safe" layer — one person's record at a time — is ever visible to the AI. (Microsoft Fabric / OneLake.)
3The specialists Language services that detect personal data, classify intent, and translate; the AI models that polish wording and create the meaning-fingerprints. (Azure AI services.)
4The conductor The chain runtime you'll watch today: runs the seven stops, enforces the checkpoint, writes every receipt. (Built on the Microsoft Agent Framework.)
5The controls Identity (who may see what — Microsoft Entra), data governance and lineage (Microsoft Purview), monitoring, and the tamper-evident audit store.
6Where you meet it Copilot in Teams for employees, the console for operators, approval cards in Teams for checkpoints, Power BI dashboards for the numbers Sofia and the CHRO watch.

Three things to retain: the AI never touches raw personal data (layer 2 masks it first); it never acts without the checkpoint (layer 4 enforces it); and everything it does is observable (layer 5 records it). Ask your instructor to show the full technical diagram if your table wants to go deeper.

Where employees meet it

What "an agent in M365 Copilot" actually means

M365 Copilot is Microsoft's AI assistant inside Teams, Outlook, and Office. Companies can add their own declarative agents to it — think of one as a specialist colleague inside Copilot with three things:

  1. A job description (its "instructions"): ours says — route each HR question to the right scenario; never answer HR policy from your own general knowledge; keep every citation; and report checkpoints honestly, including escalations.
  2. Conversation starters: nine suggested questions — one per scenario — so anyone can try it without knowing what to ask.
  3. A connection to the chain — the agent doesn't answer by itself; it calls the same governed process you'll watch in the console. Copilot is the doorway, not the brain.
Why this matters to HR: the honesty rules live in the agent's written instructions and in the chain's machinery — not in a model's goodwill. When procurement or works councils ask "how do you know it won't make things up about HR policy?", the answer is: it is not permitted to answer from memory, and here is the receipt trail that proves what it did instead.

The same package can be tested in our environment with synthetic data, then deployed at a client site against their systems — the process, checkpoints, and audit trail travel with it.

Activity 4 · Design your own · 30 min · pen and paper

Be the process owner: design scenario #10

In pairs: choose an everyday HR request this module doesn't cover — holiday calendar questions, expense-policy Q&A, "who do I ask about X", onboarding buddy FAQ, badge replacement… and design its chain on one page:

  1. The five slots: objective · use case · person served (asker + measure owner) · the chain · measure moved (invent a plausible before → after).
  2. The seven stops: what happens at each — especially stop 3: which personal facts and which exceptions matter?
  3. The checkpoint setting — touch-free, confirm, escalate, or approve — and one sentence naming the concrete harm that justifies your choice. "It feels safer" doesn't count.
  4. The library: which 2–3 documents must exist for grounding? What's deliberately excluded and routed to a human (use your Activity 2 list)?
  5. The honest failure: write the exact sentence the agent should say when it can't answer your scenario.
another pair reviews your page and attacks the checkpoint choice; you defend it with the harm scenario, not with vibes. Then swap.

Reference

Glossary — the ten words to keep

TermPlain meaning
AgentAn AI worker with a defined job, defined tools, and defined limits — not a general chatbot.
Scenario chainThe governed, repeatable sequence of steps a request travels — the thing you actually trust and measure.
GroundingAnswering only from approved documents and records, with the source quoted. The opposite of "from memory".
HallucinationA fluent answer invented by a model. Grounding + the honesty rules are the countermeasure.
Checkpoint (HITL gate)The fixed step where human oversight applies — always step 16 of the enterprise blueprint. Four settings: touch-free, confirm, escalate, approve.
EscalationA confident hand-over to the right human team with full context attached. A success, not a failure.
LedgerThe 14-fact receipt each step writes — the audit trail that lets anyone replay an answer.
Vector / meaning-fingerprintA mathematical summary of a passage that lets search match by meaning, not just by shared words.
KPIThe named measure a named person owns — deflection, first-contact resolution, handle time, satisfaction.
Declarative agent (M365 Copilot)A specialist added to Copilot: written instructions + starter questions + a connection to a governed process.
